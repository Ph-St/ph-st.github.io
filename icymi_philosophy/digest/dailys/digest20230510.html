@KPED

I think this is underappreciated primarily because GPT is far better read than humans. We easily mistake surprise for creativity, and anything that GPT makes at present levels of tech is just clever regurgitation and obfuscation of the work of others that most have never seen so will not recognize as repetition.

This irony is that contributors will not be credited even though their influence is essential and even though their livelihoods are now threatened.

Human authors credit one another for ideas so we more easily see the pedigrees and new contributions.

Nor do we place value we should on the effect of scarcity. GPT builds on all works but in doing so is not at risk of differently inventing because its job is not to invent, to build up from the small, but to pare down from the large.

As the old elephant joke goes: How do you make a statue of an elephant? You get a big block of stone and chip away everything that does not look like an elephant. The humor, we all know intuitively, is that few or none work this way. We build from below, we don't start with many already-answers and sift.

There's little doubt GPT can make enough surprises this way to fill a lifetime. But is there nothing to be said about surprises to all humanity? Things it cannot pare down from because it has no one's already-conception to whittle down and stylistically obfuscate. It will have no way to know what it lacks, or to care. Soon, with enough reliance on it, neither may we...

Not to mention it aims to make things more efficient. But was that ever the purpose of art? I'm not in the biz, but I struggle to recall an art review that ever praised an artist for their efficiency as a primary criterion of goodness.

ChatGPT probably knows one, by which I mean it can probably paraphrase a human having known one. But it's surely not common. And art has higher purpose, except perhaps in commerce. Add this to the reasons government should not run like a business.

I love clever uses of incentives and tech: Cities are using traffic lights near schools that start red and turn green if an approaching car isn't speeding.

The average speed on the road almost immediately dropped to the speed limit as people learned the rules.

Instead of punishing people with tickets after the fact, it creates the behavior the city wants.
