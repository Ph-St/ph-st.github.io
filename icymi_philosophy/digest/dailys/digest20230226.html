Wayne Wu

@attninaction@neuromatch.social

What is attention?

This is a fraught question, but it actually has a simple answer whose virtue is that every scientist of attention accepts it. This answer identifies a common ground conception of attention (to use a linguistic notion), what is in the background when we talk to each other about attention. The problem is that it has stayed too far in the background.

Here’s the definition: a subject's attending to X is the subject's mentally selecting a target to respond to. Actually, the entailed sufficient condition is enough for the science of attention.

Why is this common ground? Because our experimental methods are built on it . If so, then we’re all committed to this account just by being committed to the standard experimental paradigms.

Studying attention requires us to set or direct an experimental subject’s attention. Everyone knows how to do this. A good task requires as a necessary condition of correct task performance that a subject mentally select (e.g. visually, cognitively) a target to respond in the way instructed. That is how we control attention so as to study a capacity that is uniformly implemented trial to trial (or we can measure this by measuring performance).

This is a methodological assumption, a regulative principle. It provides a basis for constructing new paradigms but is built into how we study attention. When task instructions set mental (e.g. visual) selection this way, they set attention. Our instructions just to apply the sufficient condition.

Nicely, It also is an entailment of James’ account of attention years ago. Notice that since the sufficient condition is methodologically assumed, it is what actually anchors all the various studies of attention since the 1950s and why we’ve actually been studying the same thing even though, when we try, we give different “definitions” of attention.

The critical thing: for cognitive neuroscience, it makes attention an aspect of mind that we are trying to explain. Attention is not, for cognitive neuroscience, an explainer. It is not a cause. It does not cause neural modulations though of course, it is constituted by certain neural changes.

Compare seeing which stands at the same level as attention. Seeing is what we are trying to explain in vision science, it is not an explainer. That’s why we can speak of visually attending and seeing attentively together. The phenomena (and concepts) belong together. But no one in vision science says seeing causes the modulates neural activity. That would get the causal influence backwards. We should recognize that this would be true of attention.

What's problematic conceptually/theoretically is that people over time have gone beyond the common ground, multiplying characterizations of "attention" and creating theoretical ambiguity and, indeed, generating contradictions.
