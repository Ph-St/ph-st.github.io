Kent Pitman

@kentpitman@climatejustice.social

We need to reconsider the rules of judging incitement in the era of viral media.

Wikipedia says: "The two legal prongs that constitute incitement of imminent lawless action are as follows: Advocacy of force or criminal activity does not receive First Amendment protections if (1) the advocacy is directed to inciting or producing imminent lawless action, and (2) is likely to incite or produce such action."

However, time is twisty on the internet. It splits into pieces experienced differently by different viewers. It loops back and is re-experienced in a way that is intensifying. It holds still while others show up to see it later.

The standard cannot reasonably be whether action imminently follows the saying, but rather the viewing or the re-viewing, or else the action is both more potent as it becomes more invisible to law, most viewings being ultimately not in the moment but later.

Detractors will point to other acts of positive effect that likewise operate in this way. But such what-about-ism does not undo the truth of what I've said. There are two prongs of the incitement charge. The "imminent" standard is more complex in the modern world. But likely to yield bad action still matters.

Democrats and ESPECIALLY Democrat leaders do not stand up with bats or guns while making allegedly metaphorical or joking hints that violence is good or to be rewarded or forgiven. Democrats do not organize field trips of their fellows to comfort those who have taken up arms against democratic process, nor do they hint of medals or pardons.

But GOP leaders do. GOP egging on off violence lingers and gets replayed and distilled in ways they must be held accountable for.

We must not be made timid by claims that aggressive prosecution is unprecedented. It is the threat that is unprecedented. As with Climate Change, we must be clear that proportional responses to unprecedented challenges are not radical but simply necessary. Inaction in the face of unprecedented threat is radical.

In this case, social media presents new threats to democratic society by making incitement more potent. We must not let this increased potency, through its complex delivery mechanisms, become a defense against the crime itself. It is not radical but common sense to see the effect it is having. If we do not prosecute ever-more-routine incitements by GOP leaders, it will be their roadmap to dismantling democracy and civilized society.
