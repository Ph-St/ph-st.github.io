Andrei A. Klishin

@aklishin@fediphilosophy.org

Today I'm sharing the next chapter of studies of distributed system design through the lens of statistical physics. A particularly common bane of distributed systems is the spatial clustering of elements that makes the system more vulnerable to both internal failures and external attacks. We again start with a naval ship example and then generalize the result. We show that when system elements effectively attract each other to minimize routing cost, they inevitably form clusters. When the elements repel each other to maximize routing flexibility, they still form clusters. Right in between the two regimes, clusters can be avoided only at the expense of high design variability. There is therefore no optimal algorithm that would avoid clusters in distributed systems at no cost, in the spirit of Wolpert and Macready 1990s results on "no free lunch" in search and optimization. Mathematically, this work again relies on tensor network contractions, and we release it alongside with Lachesis, a package for arbitrary tensor network computations based on Google's TensorNetwork backend.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.05196
https://zenodo.org/record/8088164
