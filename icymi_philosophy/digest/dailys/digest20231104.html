I'm reading Marx's discussion of wages today (Capital Vol. 1 Chapter 19)

It's common to say that wages are paid in return for labour carried out. But Marx argues this is a deceptive way to talk about it. It makes it seem like the value of labour done = the wages you are paid

In fact, your labour-power is bought for the day and, spending this power all day, you will produce value that surpasses the value of the wages you were paid

Surplus value is therefore unpaid labour

Colin Gordon

@csgordon@discuss.systems

I keep watching these incidents of using AI to pick news stories or recommend sources and other things and inevitably recommending conspiracy theories and similar ... I can't help but think of all the stuff that was repeatedly drilled into me throughout middle school and high school about finding sources: a required part of evaluating any source is evaluating who wrote the source, what their (not necessarily formal) qualifications are for a topic, and what their ulterior motivations might be. This is a critical part of considering any source for any purpose. I had to do multiple assignments assessing source reliability, every year, for a good 6-7 years. And I'm continually puzzled by just how little basically anyone seems to care about this these days.

That kind of information is literally not part of the training data for these systems. Microsoft spent millions of dollars on this replacement. So throughout the process, no decision makers thought it was important enough to even analyze what the humans they were replacing *actually did* when designing the system, and instead just presumed (1) that it's feasible to automate whatever human judgement was normally involved (this seems like a dubious assumption), and (2) that whatever training data they could get without much work (read: without asking experts what was relevant and/or getting expert-provided data) would be sufficient for achieving this. Then they never thought to confirm that this data actually included information used by humans in do this job. A common response to this kind of critique is that "it's just the training data" but it's a whole sociotechnical system that picked *this* data after inadequate research and planning and decided to stick with it.

This sort of thing is why I increasingly think undergrad CS and SE students should be strongly encouraged or even required to take STS courses.
