Preston Werner

@pjw@fediphilosophy.org

On and off for the last 15 years I have had impostor syndrome. First as an academic, and more recently as an activist.
I still deal with these regularly - I know that many many people do.

One thing I'm realizing in the past months is that impostor syndrome isn't merely a phenomenon that can hold you back personally and professionally, but also in terms of living with integrity and virtue.
I often also struggle with valuing myself (that's a different issue altogether), and I am not an extremely ambitious person from a professional standpoint. But I do deeply want to think of myself as a decent person who is willing to use what little power I have to try to inform and effect change. And I realize that my impostor syndrome is also holding me back in this area, to my great disappointment.

I pass along this note because you too may be in this position - in need of a more altruistic reason to work on your own impostor syndrome in whatever field you might find yourself, but not sure what such a reason would look like. Being a good person sometimes involves sacrifices, and one version of that sacrifice is risking feeling stupid and embarrassed. Maybe re-framing it in this way can help someone besides me.

(I think this lesson applies to many areas of self-improvement; 'make sure your oxygen mask is on before helping others' and all that; but I'd never realized until recently that it also applies to impostor syndrome.)
