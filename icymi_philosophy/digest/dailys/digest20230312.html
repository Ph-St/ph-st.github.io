Kent Pitman

@Dianora

Alas, many philosophical problems are intimately tied up in tribal politics.

I don't find it hard to accept ideas from unpleasant sources. I'm a political independent with progressive leanings who used to (decades ago) listen to Limbaugh and the whole evening of Fox News not because I liked what I heard but because it seemed more interesting to understand why people disagreed with me than to hear repeatedly that people agree. I eventually stopped because they became repetitive and really not informative, not because I wanted to stop the flow of other ideas.

Suffice to say, I am not bothered about taking ideas from unusual places. It's source of useful power. And I"m not bothered offering uncomfortable ideas to people. I'm used to people being annoyed at that. I wish it made me more popular, but I'm resigned to the idea that truth-telling is not socially favored.

And yet, I do find that each conversation takes place in multiple spaces. Discussion of an issue becomes, by proxy, discussion of an ideology, such that it's hard lately to take a position that's unpopular, and NOT because I feel judged by my peers (whatever they are). I'm not worried about so-called wokeness holding me back, for example. But I think what passes for a political right is no longer a principled conservative movement, no longer the other side of a legitimate debate. And I need to offer it no fuel.

Instead, it is a hermit crab that has invaded the shell of a past party and is abusing what trust there ever was in that party, to destroy any notion of democracy, of free public exchange, of societal consensus-making, etc. It offers authoritarian wolvery in conservatively tailored sheep's clothing.

I wish the issue of political alignment could be factored out from other matters, but in some sense I think the idea of being able to pretend it is something you can talk about in the abstract is a luxury borne of a healthy free society. And right now society has a disease it needs to shake off.

One casualty that already upsets me is the inability to use metaphor without qualifying it. I have an essay I wrote a while back that I'd love to publish. But it ends in a suggestion that something be burned to the ground and started anew. It was just metaphorical. In healthy times there would be no question. But I actively fear that someone these days would take it literally. The idea that I should have to rewrite the ending pains me. So it sits languishing.

These are precarious times. And the chains it imposes on ordinary discourse are heavy, and impede the having of purely abstract discussions.

Amongst other things this meant that it was difficult to hear consonants in speech, effectively making me partially deaf.

I got a pair of AirPod Pro earbuds and set them up for my personal hearing needs. Later that day I went for a walk in my local woods and literally gasped out loud at hearing the birds I'd been missing for some years!

The way you set them up is buried deep in the Settings, so it's not surprising that not many people know about it. Here's where you need to go. You need to have the AirPods connected to your iPhone or iPad.

Settings
Airpods
Accessibility
Audio Accessibility Settings
Headphone Accommodations
Custom Audio Setup
Headphone Audio Customisation

You run through a couple of hearing tests, one for voice volume levels and one for best music settings. After the tests you should see a volume slider. It's important to move that to wherever you need it. Hearing sounds at all frequencies is great, but I found that I wanted a slight volume boost to enhance conversations.

After this you should be good to go, but you can always set it up again. And maybe make a note of where the settings are. (Why can't we bookmark particular settings, Apple?)

"A few points to note:
1) If you can get an audiogram (from a free hearing test?) you can input that at an early stage of the setup. I didn't have one to test but I imagine that it speeds up the whole process.

2) This process worked for me, but I'm not an audiologist, so it's your decision whether to go this route.

3) I used the AirPods Pro 2 and I don't know if this works with other, cheaper AirPods, or with earlier versions.

4) Airpods Pro 2 cost ¬£248 currently in the UK. That's a fair amount of money. Hearing aids that actually work typically cost between ¬£500 and ¬£3,000, and that's just for one. You usually get a discount for two, but it's still a lot of money. Cheaper to get an audiogram and the AirPods.

https://support.apple.com/en-gb/guide/airpods/dev85c352340/web

7) I'm going to repeat the warning that Apple (and every other manufacturer of such audio devices gives you, slightly revised for the context: Listening (to anything through AirPods) at high volumes can damage your health.
