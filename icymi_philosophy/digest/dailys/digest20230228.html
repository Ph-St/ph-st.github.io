I don't think attention is a mechanism! As I have liked saying recently, seeing isn't a mechanism either (and seeing and attending go together).

But attention is realized by neural mechanisms/computational processes.

In Heeger and Reynold's DN model, I take the attention field to be a signal that carries task relevant information, say a spatial bias due to a cue during the task/trial (assuming a top-down application of the model).

My take: the output of the model just explains the relevant measured modulation observed by electrophysiologists. You don't get attention, as something the animal is doing, until the animal is acting on something selectively, namely the task relevant stimulus. Here, the brain has to do its part and its selective processing to support task performance might depend on normalization.

So, contrast or multiplicative gain is just one part of the neuural story explaining the animals action (and in that, the animals visual attention, say). The gain modulation we record is a snapshot of neural processing while the animal is performing the learned task (i.e. is attending). None of that is attention though it might support attention. So, these are theories of the neural realization of a subject's attending. Attention, as Desimone and Duncan emphasized, is the thing that results from normalization (or biased competition).
