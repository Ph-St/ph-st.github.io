Shocking news out of the ACU about Dianoia and the humanities more broadly; I understand more than 30 FTE staff are to be made redundant across a variety of fields (most notably History and Philosophy) ‚Äì this must be close to 50% of the research active staff in humanities in the institution.

While the appalling cruelty of the ACU administration is hopefully rare, I'm afraid the cuts to humanities research are symptomatic of broader currents. With soft student demand, both domestic and international, and with both JRG and what we can foresee of the new accord strongly against any use of student fee income to cross-subsidize research, plus lots of hints that the government wants to use the mooted 'Tertiary Education Commission' to allocate places to institutions to de-fund 'low value' courses, I am very worried that ACU is the canary in the coalmine here, and that research outside ARC/NHMRC schemes is going to be increasingly hard to conduct for all of us in Australia. (For some informed predictions about the prospective impacts of the accord, see this post by Andrew Norton: https://andrewnorton.net.au/2023/07/25/for-universities-the-accord-interim-report-proposes-a-more-extreme-version-of-job-ready-graduates/)

Administrations should be resisting in favour of academic values but are capitulating, the interests of management as usual being more aligned with extracting funding from government than in advocating for the intellectual mission of a university.

I had heard a rumour that part of ACU's motivation for the Dianoia institute and the parallel institute in history was a fear that reforms were imminent to link the use of the label 'University' to a minimum threshold of research activity - a threshold ACU would not meet, having many teaching only staff and being primarily a service-teaching institution serving low research intensity degree programs. That mooted reform seems to have evaporated, and I suspect the ACU adminstration have sensed that the Federal Government sees universities as a tool for social mobility, not the increase of knowledge, and so will not care that research activity in the humanities in the institution is gutted by these proposed changes. #philosophy #higherEd
