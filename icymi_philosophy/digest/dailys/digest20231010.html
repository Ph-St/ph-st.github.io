Preston Werner

@pjw@fediphilosophy.org

I've been struggling to think of ways to contextualize what is happening now in Israel/Palestine in ways that haven't already been widely discussed by the international media.
The international analysis has done a good job of explaining Israel's policy toward Gaza, Hamas, and the Palestinians in general over the past years.

What I haven't seen mentioned is the internal Israeli politics behind this policy. The thing that is interesting about Israel's attitude toward the Palestinians is that the issue of the 'conflict'/occupation is not something that most Israelis in recent years considered when choosing who to vote for.

Making peace or ending the occupation has simply not been on the agenda one way or another, outside of the "Arab" parties, which are never allowed to be in the governing coalition anyway.

You have the Jewish right, like Netanyahu's Likud, who's policy was simply to manage the status quo. Yes, settlements continued to expand, slowly but surely shrinking Palestinian land in the West Bank. But this was not something he ran on, because this happened even in the (few!) times in recent memory that he hasn't been in power. Slow settlement expansion was the unspoken policy of all mainstream political parties.

The Jewish left, once a strong voice against the occupation, realized in recent years that this is not an issue that wins them many votes, or at least not many votes that they wouldn't win anyway. In the most recent election cycles, there was virtually *no* public debate about the occupation. If you ask a politician in one of the Jewish left parties, they will of course criticize the occupation, but if you don't ask them, it simply won't come up. They have shifted focus to more traditional leftist issues like cost of living, social services, public transportation, and so on.

Because of historic instability in Israel's internal politics, there have been 5 elections in the last 4 years. The occupation was not a major issue for any of the parties (except the Arab parties, and even then, only some of them sometimes) in any of these elections with one exception:
In the most recent election, which resulted in the current Israeli government which is considered the most far right government in Israel's history, Otzma Yehudit ("Jewish Power") party, led by Itamar Ben-Gvir, was very open about advancing settlements, crushing Palestinian resistance, forcing loyalty oaths on Palestinians living in Israel, etc. Ben-Gvir wouldn't say it in so many words but in policies he wants a single apartheid state from the river to the sea.

His party swept into office, and Netanyahu, sensing that this was the only way to form a government and become Prime MInister again, gave Ben Gvir the position of Minister of National Security.

The Israeli Jewish left has always been against the occupation, but because for many of them it doesn't affect them on a daily basis, it was not present in people's minds - remember, Israel is also a country with all of the 'ordinary' problems that any other country has as well. So it dropped off of people's radar, relatively speaking. But for the Israeli Jewish far-right, who's base are settlers in the West Bank, the occupation is in their face on a daily basis. For them it *is* a 'kitchen table' issue.

Hamas' despicable actions, which have directly or indirectly affected everyone living in Israel (and that includes Palestinian citizens, who have also been killed), is obviously going to force this issue. But the only narrative that the mainstream Israeli has to grab onto is the one that was being pushed by the right in recent years. And, sorry for all you armchair international relations Tooters, but this is not an easy time for an Israeli to do complicated political analysis, so that's where people's heads are going.
