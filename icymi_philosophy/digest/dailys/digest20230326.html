1. The authors are not comparing their "new model" to the current value prediction error (often called "reward prediction error") models. They compare it to a strawman model based on discrete states that no one in the field has defended for decades. Current models based on complex states with multiple discounting functions look a lot closer to what they are selling.

2. A large key to their argument lies in the observation that dopamine does not diminish at the cues after extinction Fig 4J, but this is an experiment that's been run dozens of times with other results, where other people find it does. I do not know why their mice don't look like everyone else's.

3. Moreover, the final test (Fig5D) shows results inconsistent with either theory: cue response is half what ANCCR predicts and opposite what their RPE model predicts, but reward response is consistent with RPE models, not ANCCR.

This is a much smaller change to the current classic VPE/RPE views on dopamine than is being sold in this paper.

It is too bad that people have to sell their work as "demolishing old work" instead of as "building on it" or even as "why did we see this difference?" in order to get into the fancy GlamourMags. It's good to get these published, but a more nuanced discussion would have been a more useful contribution to the literature.
