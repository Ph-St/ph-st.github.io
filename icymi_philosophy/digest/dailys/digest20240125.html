It's been so long since I originally heard this that I forgot who raised it to me, so I want to be clear that this is not my original argument. But I find it compelling, and I'm wondering what others think, as well as if anyone knows where a similar line of thought is given:

You've heard of the Demandingness Objection to moral theories, how about the Not-Demanding-Enough Objection:

1. The world is filled with all kinds of terrible and preventably terrible things.
2. It is a serious cost for a moral theory to entail that these terrible and preventably terrible things do not impose obligations on individuals to mitigate those terrible things.
So,
3. Any moral theory which is not at least somewhat revisionary of ordinary and common sense practices faces a serious cost, and should, other things being equal, be rejected.
