Kent Pitman

@kentpitman@climatejustice.social

It's encouraging to see nations starting to acknowledge their climate responsibility, at least in Europe. The recent court ruling saying Switzerland isn't doing its part on Climate is not a total fix, but a step along the path.

I wish something so simple would work in the US. So much to do, so little time.

While European countries disagree on many political details, it seems at least from where I sit like they acknowledge there IS a problem and a need for action at a systematic level.

In the US, I feel like we're stuck on the idea that markets and rugged individualism will save us. The trouble is that markets are too full of squirrels (distractions, other ways to make profit). Rather than climate goals driving short term priority, money does.

I understand the argument for why capitalism is theoretically capable of doing some things efficiently, but that theory only works in practice in a distraction-free environment. Taxes and incentives could reduce the distractions. But the same companies telling us capitalism is up to the task are the ones using regulatory capture to keep us from turning the systematic knobs that would allow it to actually accomplish the task.

In the US, arguments are made that climate policy hurts markets or hurts the economy.

By "hurt markets", they mean hurt the health of companies committed to injuring the climate. We need metrics that see that as success, not failure.

By "hurt the economy", they mean we're not growing enough, at a time when sustainability or degrowth are what we should be valuing. Again a problem with metrics. (The idea that perpetual growth in a finite world is an apparent goal horrifies me.)

If we don't know how to recognize and measure progress, the optimization engine that is capitalism cannot usefully function. Efficiency is of no value when the engine itself is pointed in the wrong direction.

Boundary and other governing conditions are essential for any optimization engine, to avoid nonsense solutions. Refusing to set them seems to me to 'void the manufacturer's warranty'. Even Adam Smith argued that to have morality in capitalism, it had to be encoded in law, that markets would not discover morality, but instead would tend toward selfish tyranny. That we therefore let these selfish tyrants have the last word on what's good for capitalism seems ... Ill-advised ... and, in this case, also suicidal for humanity.

If courts can fix this, even just in Europe, I'm all for it. I'm less optimistic in the US. Our courts in the US have become political.

And for some reason climate science and human survival have become political, too.

It makes me sad.

#Climate #capitalism #ClimateLaw #EuropeanClimateLaw
