Preston Werner

@pjw@fediphilosophy.org

The Israeli government, and much of the media in following, has fixated on the elimination of Hamas. Let's set aside for the moment whether this is a realistic goal, and ask the merits of achieving such a goal.
Now, stated as "we want to eliminate Hamas", given that Hamas is a terrorist organization, this frames the debate in a way that makes it sound as though this is obviously a great goal in theory. Of course we might ask about how to ethically enact such a goal given the amount of civilian casualties this would (and has) resulted in - even assuming, incorrectly in my opinion, that Israel has tried to minimize civilian casualties in any serious way.

However, if eliminating Hamas is a worthy goal, it isn't because being an organization called "Hamas" is the problem. If eliminating Hamas is a worthy goal, it's because terrorism and extremism which motivate and glorify the brutal killing of innocent people are so abhorrent and inconsistent with our values that we must do our best to eliminate terrorism and extremism.
So instead of framing the question as "Can Israel achieve its goal of eliminating Hamas by taking actions x, y, and z in Gaza?"
The real question is
"Can Israel achieve its goal of eliminating extremism and terrorist ideology by taking actions x, y, and z in Gaza?"

And once we stop thinking in terms of one terror organization in particular and start thinking about extremism and terror ideology in general, the justification of performing actions which time and time again have been shown to breed and encourage extremism and terrorism as a way to eliminate extremism and terrorism becomes much shakier.

Peter Finocchiaro

I'm very happy to say that my new paper with Tim Perrine is now free to access at Philosophical Psychology, courtesy of Lex Academic and their essay prize for understanding discrimination. You can find it here: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09515089.2023.2284243

In the paper, Tim and I examine the role of English in academic philosophy. We argue (among other things) that the increasing use of English has led to linguistic injustices. We then offer an analysis of these injustices using the framework of distributive epistemic injustice. We end by suggesting a few proposals for addressing these injustices.

This project is very near and dear to my heart. It synthesizes so many of the experiences that I've had since moving to Wuhan over 5 years ago. I am especially appreciative of my Chinese students and colleagues who have confronted these injustices in their own lives. I hope we can work together to make philosophy better for them and for everyone.
