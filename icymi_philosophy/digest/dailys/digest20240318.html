"Problems of the constitution of mathematical objects: phenomenology between realism and idealism".

Abstract:

The ideal objects with which mathematics deals seem to be endowed with omnitemporal validity (for any possible time) and omnisubjective validity (for anyone). How can these two initial observations be reconciled? Should we accept the realist thesis according to which mathematical idealities have a being in themselves, independent of any thinking subject and of any temporality? We will attempt to unfold this problem and develop a coherent response by drawing on the resources of Husserlian conceptuality, while questioning his fundamental theses. Our starting point will be the concept of transcendental constitution, which we will apply to mathematical objects and examine the thesis of transcendental idealism. Using the example of the arithmetical continuum as defined by Dedekind, we will examine the thesis that irrationals are created by acts of thought. We will then analyse the Husserlian method of analysing strata of meaning, to show how it makes room for a wide range of ontological theses. We conclude by examining the meaning of the omnitemporality of mathematical objects.
