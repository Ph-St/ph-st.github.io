Some more thoughts on the ACU situation, posted in response to this petition:

The establishment of Dianoia and the other institutes was a rare piece of good news for the humanities in Australia in recent years. I was a strong supporter of the project from the start; several of my former students went on to the PhD program, where I knew they could get access to research supervisors second to none in Australasia. And as editor of a major journal in the field, I recruited a number of Dianoia researchers to the editorial team, amazed and grateful and the philosophical talent Dianoia had brought to Australia. This is all to be lost, for absolutely no good reason. What a sad sad end to a beautiful‚Äîand absolutely viable‚Äîvision.

(Yes there are budgetary issues in research funding in all fields in Australia, and flat domestic student numbers are challenging all institutions. But philosophy undergraduates are very lucrative after the JRG reforms, and the simple thought that it might be possible to generate more revenue by directing some of the extraordinary philosophical talent available at ACU towards teaching undergraduates seems to have been beyond the clearly cursory exploration of possibilities undertaken by those designing the change proposal.)

This is particularly heartbreaking as Dianoia demonstrated, very clearly, that research excellence could be achieved rapidly, at scale, and very cheaply ‚Äì and if ACU could do it, it was clear any institution who wanted to improve their research intensity could do it. A newer instititution that could never hope to compete with a Go8 in STEM research facilities showed it could nevertheless boost their research performance above most Go8s in a couple of years with very limited but targeted and effective investment in humanities. This remains true! But I fear that the blinkered thinking and lack of flexibility demonstrated by the senior administration at ACU will give a misleading impression to other universities that this model isn't viable. Again, ACU's decision not only harms itself, but harms the prospects of the humanities across Australia, and engenders a generalized suspicion of Australian jobs and working conditions in international academics we'd love to hire. Short-sighted and obtuse doesn't begin to cover it, without even mentioning the gratuitous cruelty to staff and HDR candidates that the proposal displays. It's hard to think of a more callous decision in Australian HE.

It seems impossible at this point that staff and students at Dianoia could ever trust the administration at ACU again; even if this proposal were completely taken off the table at this point it would be rational to fear its revival at any budgetary setback. Staff would be certain to take outside offers; HDR candidates would be transferring to more reliable institutions, or forced to leave once supervisory expertise evaporates. I see no upside from this point; whatever happens, something great has been tarnished and destroyed, causing great and avoidable harm to individuals along the way. A sad day for the humanities in Australia, and a black stain on ACU that will not even be remedied by the wholesale replacement of the senior management. (Though that would be a welcome start. Time for the ACU Senate to take their obligation to the future of the university seriously.)
